# LinkedIn Voice Adaptation Guide

This document explains how to translate a brand's voice profile into LinkedIn-appropriate content.

---

## The LinkedIn Tone Spectrum

LinkedIn content lives in a specific register: professional enough to be taken seriously, personal enough to feel human. The platform rewards authenticity and penalizes corporate-speak.

| Too Corporate | Sweet Spot | Too Casual |
|---|---|---|
| "We are pleased to announce the strategic acquisition of..." | "We just acquired [Company]. Here's why, and what changes for you." | "lol we bought a company, wild times" |
| "Leveraging our synergistic capabilities..." | "We combined our API with their data pipeline. The result:" | "we smashed the two things together and it works great ngl" |
| "It is with great enthusiasm that I share..." | "Something I learned this week that changed how I think about hiring:" | "okay hot take incoming about hiring lmao" |

## Translating Brand Voice to LinkedIn

### If the Brand Voice Is Formal
- Drop one formality level for LinkedIn
- Replace third person with first person where appropriate
- Keep the vocabulary precise but make the sentence structure more conversational
- Use contractions
- Maintain authority through insight, not through formal language

### If the Brand Voice Is Casual
- Raise one formality level for LinkedIn
- Keep the personality but add professional framing
- Ground opinions in experience and evidence, not just vibes
- Avoid slang that might not translate across industries or cultures

### If the Brand Voice Is Technical
- Keep the technical depth (LinkedIn's professional audience appreciates it)
- Add business or career context: why should a non-technical decision-maker care?
- Use analogies to bridge technical concepts for a broader audience
- Lead with the "so what" before going deep

### If the Brand Voice Is Warm/Friendly
- This translates well to LinkedIn. Lean into it.
- Add specificity to keep warmth from becoming vagueness
- Use personal anecdotes and name real people (with permission)
- Warmth on LinkedIn means generosity with insights, not cheerfulness

## The "I" Voice on LinkedIn

LinkedIn is one of the few professional platforms where first person singular is powerful. Guidelines:

**When to use "I":**
- Sharing personal experiences and lessons learned
- Stating opinions you personally hold
- Describing decisions you made and their outcomes

**When to use "we":**
- Talking about team or company achievements
- Describing collaborative processes
- Sharing company-level results

**When to use "you":**
- Giving advice
- Framing insights as directly applicable to the reader
- Asking questions

**Default:** Start with "I" for the story, shift to "you" for the takeaway.

## Storytelling on LinkedIn

LinkedIn's algorithm favors posts that keep people reading. Storytelling is the most reliable way to achieve this.

### Story Structure for LinkedIn
1. **Hook**: The most surprising or valuable part of the story (first line)
2. **Context**: Brief setup (2-3 short paragraphs)
3. **Tension**: The challenge, conflict, or decision point
4. **Resolution**: What happened
5. **Lesson**: The transferable insight

### Story Authenticity Markers
- Specific dates, numbers, or names
- Admission of mistakes or uncertainty
- Details only someone who was there would know
- Emotional honesty without melodrama

## What LinkedIn's Algorithm Rewards

Understanding the platform's incentives helps shape voice:

- **Dwell time**: Longer reading time signals quality. Write posts worth reading slowly.
- **Comments over likes**: Posts that generate discussion outperform posts that generate passive likes. End with genuine questions.
- **Native content**: Text posts and carousels outperform link shares. If sharing a link, add substantial commentary.
- **Early engagement**: The first hour matters. Write for your most engaged connections first.

---

## Voice Consistency Across Platforms

If the user is also creating content for X, blog, or other platforms:

- LinkedIn posts should feel like the same person speaking at a professional conference, not reading from a teleprompter
- The vocabulary can shift between platforms, but the core perspective and personality should remain consistent
- A blog post's insight can become a LinkedIn post, but it needs to be rewritten for the platform, not copy-pasted
