# LinkedIn Post Generation

## Before You Start

1. **Check for brand voice**: Read `brand/VOICE.md` if it exists. Adapt the brand voice for LinkedIn's professional-but-personal tone. If no voice file exists, default to professional, authentic, and insight-driven.

2. **Determine post type**: Choose the format that fits the content:
   - **Personal Story** -- Lessons from experience (`templates/personal-story.template`)
   - **Professional Insight** -- Frameworks, observations, advice (`templates/professional-insight.template`)
   - **Contrarian Take** -- Challenging industry assumptions (`templates/contrarian-take.template`)

3. **Gather inputs**: Topic, key message, target audience (role/seniority), any data or examples to include.

## LinkedIn's Unique Culture

LinkedIn sits in a specific cultural space. Understand it before writing:

**What works:**
- Professional experiences told as personal stories
- Specific, actionable advice grounded in real experience
- Vulnerable honesty about failures and lessons learned
- Frameworks and mental models that help people think
- Data-backed observations about industry trends
- Asking genuine questions that invite thoughtful discussion

**What doesn't work:**
- Corporate press releases disguised as personal posts
- Vague inspirational platitudes ("Success is a journey, not a destination")
- Humblebragging ("I'm so humbled to announce...")
- Engagement bait without substance ("Agree?" "Thoughts?")
- Reposting motivational quotes with "This." as your commentary
- Multi-paragraph LinkedIn posts that are actually just ads

**The core rule:** LinkedIn rewards posts that make the reader think, learn, or reflect. If your post does none of these, rewrite it.

## Post Structure

LinkedIn's algorithm and reading experience reward a specific structure:

### Hook Line (First 1-2 Lines)
- This is the only thing visible before "...see more"
- It must create enough curiosity or value-promise to earn the click
- Strong hooks: bold claim, surprising observation, specific result, a question
- Weak hooks: "I'm excited to share...", "Big news!", generic statements

### Body (Short Paragraphs with White Space)
- Keep paragraphs to 1-3 sentences max
- Use line breaks between paragraphs (LinkedIn's mobile experience demands white space)
- One idea per paragraph
- Build toward your main insight
- Include at least one specific detail: a number, a name, a date, a quote

### CTA / Close
- End with a question that invites genuine discussion (not "Agree?")
- Or end with a specific actionable takeaway
- Or end with a forward-looking thought

## Emoji Usage

LinkedIn has its own emoji culture. Rules:

**Acceptable:**
- Bullet point substitutes (use sparingly, max 3-5 in a post)
- A single emoji to punctuate a key line
- Section markers in list-heavy posts

**Not acceptable:**
- Every line starting with an emoji
- Emoji walls or rainbow emoji sequences
- Clapping hands between every word
- Using emojis as a substitute for substance

**Default:** When in doubt, skip the emojis. Clean text with strong ideas performs better than decorated text with weak ideas.

## LinkedIn Anti-Slop

These patterns are rampant on LinkedIn. Avoid all of them.

### Phrases to Never Write
- "I'm humbled to announce..."
- "I'm thrilled to share..."
- "Agree?"
- "Thoughts?"
- "This." (as a comment on a repost)
- "If you're not doing X, you're falling behind"
- "In today's rapidly changing landscape..."
- "Here's the thing about {topic}..."
- "Let that sink in."
- "Read that again."
- "I don't usually post about this, but..."

### Structural Patterns to Avoid
- The "I got fired, and it was the best thing that ever happened" formula (unless it genuinely was, with specific details)
- Fake dialogue posts ("Interviewer: Why should we hire you? Me: Because I...")
- "Day 1 vs Day 365" transformation posts without substance
- Listing obvious advice as if it's revolutionary
- Ending every post with a self-promotional CTA disguised as generosity

### What to Do Instead
- Share a specific moment, decision, or observation
- Include details only you would know (this proves authenticity)
- Let the reader draw their own conclusions from your story
- If you have advice, ground it in a concrete experience, not abstract wisdom

## Output Checklist

Before delivering a LinkedIn post:

- [ ] Hook line earns the "...see more" click
- [ ] Paragraphs are short (1-3 sentences) with white space between them
- [ ] At least one specific detail (number, name, date, quote)
- [ ] No LinkedIn slop phrases (check the full list above)
- [ ] Voice is professional but personal (not corporate, not casual)
- [ ] Post makes the reader think, learn, or reflect
- [ ] Closing invites genuine engagement or delivers a takeaway
- [ ] Emoji usage is restrained and purposeful (or absent)
- [ ] Length is appropriate: 150-300 words for most posts (LinkedIn penalizes very long posts in the algorithm)
- [ ] Brand voice is adapted for LinkedIn's culture
