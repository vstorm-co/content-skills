# Audience Questions — Deep Dive

This file extends the audience portion of Phase 1. Use it when the initial "who do you serve" answer needs more depth, or when the brand serves multiple audience segments.

---

## Why This Matters

Every content decision downstream depends on knowing the audience precisely. Vague audience definitions produce vague content. "We write for developers" tells you almost nothing. "We write for mid-level backend engineers at Series B startups who are evaluating observability tools for the first time" tells you everything.

---

## Primary Audience Segment

### Demographics & Role

**Ask:** "Let's build a detailed picture of your primary reader. What's their job title?"

**Follow-ups:**
- "How many years of experience do they typically have?"
- "What's their day-to-day actually look like? What tools are open on their screen right now?"
- "What size company do they work at? Does company size matter for your product?"
- "Do they have budget authority, or do they need to convince someone else?"

**Extract:** Title, seniority, daily workflow, company size, buying power.

### Pain Points

**Ask:** "What's the problem that keeps this person up at night -- the one your product solves?"

**Probe for specificity:**
- If they say "efficiency" -> "Efficiency at what? What task takes too long right now?"
- If they say "scaling" -> "Scaling what exactly? Users? Data? Team? Revenue?"
- If they say "complexity" -> "Walk me through the most complicated part of their current workflow."

**Follow-ups:**
- "What have they already tried to solve this? Why didn't it work?"
- "How urgent is this problem? Is it a 'nice to have' or a 'the CEO is asking about this'?"
- "What happens if they do nothing? What's the cost of inaction?"

**Extract:** Primary pain point, urgency level, failed alternatives, cost of inaction.

### Goals & Aspirations

**Ask:** "Beyond solving the immediate problem, what does success look like for this person in 6-12 months?"

**Probe:**
- "What would their boss congratulate them for?"
- "What would they brag about at a conference?"
- "Is there a metric they're measured on that your product directly impacts?"

**Extract:** Short-term and long-term goals, success metrics, career aspirations relevant to the product.

### Knowledge Level

**Ask:** "How much does your ideal reader already know about your space? Are they a beginner, intermediate, or expert?"

**Probe:**
- "Do they know the terminology, or do you need to define terms?"
- "Have they used similar products before, or is this category new to them?"
- "When they Google their problem, what words do they search for?"

**Extract:** Technical sophistication, vocabulary expectations, search behavior.

### Where They Hang Out

**Ask:** "Where does this person go to learn and stay current? What do they read, watch, or listen to?"

**Probe:**
- "Any specific newsletters, podcasts, YouTube channels, or communities?"
- "Do they prefer long reads, short posts, video, or audio?"
- "Are they more Twitter/X, LinkedIn, Reddit, or something else entirely?"

**Extract:** Media consumption habits, preferred content formats, community affiliations.

## Secondary Audience Segments

**Ask:** "Is there a second audience you care about -- someone else who reads your content but isn't the primary target?"

Common secondary audiences:
- Decision-makers who approve purchases (different from end users)
- Developers who implement vs. managers who evaluate
- Existing customers vs. prospects
- Industry analysts or press

For each secondary segment, capture at minimum:
- Who they are (title/role)
- How their needs differ from the primary audience
- What content specifically serves them
- How much of your total content effort they should receive (rough percentage)

---

## Anti-Audience

**Ask:** "Is there anyone you explicitly do NOT want reading your content? Or someone who reads it and gets the wrong idea?"

This question surfaces important positioning choices:
- "We're not for enterprise" tells you to avoid enterprise jargon
- "We're not for beginners" tells you the baseline knowledge to assume
- "We're not for people who want the cheapest option" tells you to lean into value, not price

**Extract:** Who is out of scope and why. This is just as valuable as knowing who is in scope.

---

## The Audience Test

After this section, you should be able to answer all of these:

1. If this person walked into a coffee shop, could you spot them? (You should know enough to describe their world.)
2. If you wrote a headline, would they click it? (You should know their pain points and vocabulary.)
3. If they read your content, would they share it? (You should know where they hang out and what they value.)
4. If they showed it to a colleague, would the colleague also find it relevant? (You should know the team dynamic.)

If any answer is "I'm not sure," go back and ask more questions.
