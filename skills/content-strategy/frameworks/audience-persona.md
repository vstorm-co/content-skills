# Audience Persona Framework

## Purpose

An audience persona is a detailed, specific description of a representative member of your target audience. It exists to keep content focused on real human needs rather than abstract "target markets."

A good persona is specific enough to guide content decisions. "Developers" is not a persona. "Senior backend engineers at Series B startups who are evaluating observability tools" is closer.

---

## Persona Template

Use this template for each audience segment. Fill in with specifics, not generalities.

```
## Persona: [Name / Label]

### Demographics
- **Role**: [Job title and function]
- **Seniority**: [Junior / Mid / Senior / Lead / Executive]
- **Company profile**: [Size, stage, industry]
- **Experience**: [Years in role, career trajectory]
- **Location/timezone**: [If relevant to content timing or cultural context]

### Psychographics
- **Values**: [What do they care about professionally? Quality? Speed? Innovation? Stability?]
- **Identity**: [How do they see themselves? "Pragmatic engineer," "creative problem solver," "data-driven leader"]
- **Worldview**: [Their mental model of how their industry/domain works]
- **Frustrations**: [What makes them angry or cynical about their field?]
- **Aspirations**: [Where do they want to be in 2-3 years?]

### Information Diet
- **Publications**: [Blogs, newsletters, magazines they read]
- **Social platforms**: [Where they spend time -- X, LinkedIn, Reddit, HN, Discord, Slack communities]
- **Influencers**: [People they follow and respect]
- **Formats**: [Do they prefer long reads? Short threads? Video? Podcasts?]
- **Consumption time**: [When do they consume content? Morning commute? Lunch? Weekend deep reads?]

### Pain Points
1. [Specific problem #1 -- be concrete]
2. [Specific problem #2]
3. [Specific problem #3]

### Current Beliefs
- [What they currently believe about your topic]
- [Misconceptions or outdated mental models]
- [What they think they need vs. what they actually need]

### Desired Beliefs
- [What you want them to believe after consuming your content]
- [The "aha moment" you are trying to create]

### Content Triggers
- [Situations that cause them to seek content like yours]
- [Keywords they search for]
- [Questions they ask in communities]

### Decision Factors
- [What influences their decisions? Peer recommendations? Data? Case studies?]
- [Who else is involved in their decisions? (Boss, team, procurement)]
- [What objections will they have?]
```

## Building a Persona

### Step 1: Start with Real Data

The best personas come from real conversations and data, not imagination:

- **Customer interviews**: Even 5 conversations reveal patterns
- **Support tickets**: What questions do people actually ask?
- **Community discussions**: What do they talk about in Reddit, Discord, or Slack?
- **Analytics**: What content already resonates? What do they search for?
- **Sales conversations**: What objections come up? What problems do prospects describe?

If you do not have access to real data, be explicit that the persona is hypothetical and should be validated.

### Step 2: Find the Specifics

Push past generic descriptions:

| Generic (Useless) | Specific (Useful) |
|---|---|
| "Wants to stay up to date" | "Reads Hacker News at lunch, skims titles, opens 2-3 articles" |
| "Cares about quality" | "Will reject a tool if the docs have typos" |
| "Technical decision maker" | "Evaluates tools by building a POC over a weekend, then writes an internal RFC" |
| "Active on social media" | "Posts on LinkedIn 2x/week, mostly reshares with commentary" |

### Step 3: Identify the Belief Gap

The most important part of a content persona is the belief gap:

- **Current belief**: What they think today
- **Desired belief**: What you want them to think
- **The gap**: Your content strategy lives in this space

Example:
- Current: "Observability is just logging with a fancy name"
- Desired: "Observability is a fundamentally different approach that catches issues logging misses"
- Gap: Content must demonstrate (not just claim) the difference with concrete examples

### Step 4: Validate and Iterate

Personas are hypotheses. Validate them by:

- Testing content designed for the persona and measuring engagement
- Watching for comments or feedback that confirm or contradict persona assumptions
- Updating the persona quarterly as you learn more

## Multiple Personas

Most content strategies serve 2-3 personas. When you have multiple:

1. **Rank them**: Who is the primary audience? Who is secondary?
2. **Map content to personas**: Not every piece serves every persona. Be explicit about which persona each content idea targets.
3. **Find overlaps**: Where do persona needs overlap? Those are your highest-leverage content opportunities.
4. **Avoid the average**: Do not write content for "the average" of your personas. Write specifically for one persona per piece.

---

## Common Mistakes

1. **Too many personas**: If you have more than 3, you probably have not made hard enough choices.
2. **Demographic-only personas**: Age, title, and company size tell you almost nothing about content preferences. Psychographics matter more.
3. **Aspirational personas**: Describing who you wish your audience were instead of who they actually are.
4. **Static personas**: Personas should evolve as you learn. Treat them as living documents.
5. **Persona as checkbox**: Building a persona and then ignoring it when creating content. Reference the persona actively in every content brief.
