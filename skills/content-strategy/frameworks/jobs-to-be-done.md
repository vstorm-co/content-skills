# Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) Framework for Content

## Core Concept

People do not consume content for its own sake. They "hire" content to do a job for them. Understanding that job is the foundation of content that resonates.

The JTBD question: **When someone reads/watches/listens to this content, what job are they hiring it to do?**

---

## Common Content Jobs

### Functional Jobs (Getting Something Done)

| Job | Example Content |
|---|---|
| Learn how to do something specific | Tutorial, how-to guide, walkthrough |
| Solve a problem I am stuck on right now | Troubleshooting guide, Stack Overflow-style post |
| Evaluate whether a tool/approach is right for me | Comparison post, honest review, case study |
| Stay current on industry changes | News roundup, trend analysis, changelog recap |
| Make a decision with confidence | Decision framework, pros/cons analysis, benchmark data |
| Build a business case for my boss | ROI analysis, case study with metrics, industry report |

### Emotional Jobs (Feeling Something)

| Job | Example Content |
|---|---|
| Feel confident that I am on the right track | Validation post, "you're not alone" narrative |
| Feel inspired to try something new | Showcase, success story, creative exploration |
| Feel smart / informed when talking to peers | Deep dive, insider knowledge, contrarian take |
| Feel less overwhelmed by complexity | Simplification post, "the only guide you need" |
| Feel part of a community | Community roundup, shared experience, inside jokes |

### Social Jobs (Being Perceived a Certain Way)

| Job | Example Content |
|---|---|
| Look knowledgeable when I share this | Data-rich insight, original research, unique angle |
| Signal that I am up-to-date | Breaking news take, early trend analysis |
| Start a conversation with peers | Provocative opinion, poll-worthy question, debate piece |
| Demonstrate expertise to my network | Advanced technical deep dive, architectural analysis |

## Applying JTBD to Content Strategy

### Step 1: Identify the Job

For each content idea, ask:

- What situation triggers someone to seek this content?
- What are they trying to accomplish?
- What would they do if this content did not exist?
- How will they know the content has done its job?

### Step 2: Assess Job Frequency and Urgency

| Dimension | Question |
|---|---|
| Frequency | How often does this job arise? Daily? Monthly? Once? |
| Urgency | When the job arises, how urgently does it need to be done? |
| Alternatives | What are they currently using to get this job done? |
| Satisfaction | How satisfied are they with current alternatives? |

High-frequency, high-urgency jobs with poor current alternatives are the best content opportunities.

### Step 3: Design Content for the Job

Once you know the job:

- **Format follows function**: A "solve my problem right now" job demands a scannable, actionable format (not a 3000-word think piece). A "feel inspired" job needs narrative and emotion.
- **Depth follows need**: Give enough detail to complete the job, no more. Over-explaining a simple task is as bad as under-explaining a complex one.
- **Distribution follows context**: Where is someone when this job arises? At their desk? On their phone during a commute? In a meeting? Place the content where the job happens.

### Step 4: Validate the Job

After publishing, check whether the content actually did its job:

- Did people complete the action the content was designed to support?
- Did engagement patterns match the expected job? (e.g., high saves/bookmarks for reference content, high shares for social-signal content)
- What feedback or comments indicate the job was done (or not)?

## JTBD Brief Template

Include this in content briefs to keep the team focused on the job:

```
**Job to Be Done**: [One sentence describing the job]
**Trigger**: [What situation causes someone to need this content?]
**Current alternatives**: [How are they getting this job done today?]
**Success criteria**: [How does the reader know the content did its job?]
**Competing jobs**: [What other jobs might distract from this one?]
```

## Common Mistakes

1. **Confusing the topic with the job**: "Kubernetes" is a topic. "Understand whether I should migrate to Kubernetes" is a job.
2. **Designing for your job, not theirs**: You want brand awareness. They want to solve a problem. Design for their job; your job gets done as a side effect.
3. **Ignoring emotional and social jobs**: Most content strategies only address functional jobs. The emotional and social jobs are often what drive sharing and loyalty.
4. **One piece, many jobs**: Trying to do too many jobs in one piece of content results in doing none of them well. One piece, one primary job.
