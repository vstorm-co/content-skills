# Flat Illustration Style

## Description

Bold, geometric illustrations with clean lines, solid colors, and minimal detail. No shadows, no gradients (or very subtle ones), no textures. Feels vector-like, modern, and approachable. Think of the visual language used by Notion, Slack, or Dropbox in their marketing illustrations.

---

## Key Characteristics

- **Colors**: Bold, limited palette. 3-5 colors maximum per illustration. High contrast between elements.
- **Shapes**: Geometric and simplified. Circles, rectangles, rounded corners. Organic shapes are simplified to basic curves.
- **Shadows**: None or minimal. No drop shadows, no ambient occlusion. If depth is needed, use overlapping shapes with slight color variation.
- **Gradients**: None or very subtle (single-axis, low-contrast gradients only).
- **Detail level**: Low. Characters have simple features. Objects are schematic, not realistic.
- **Line work**: Optional. When present, consistent weight, rounded caps.
- **Perspective**: Flat (no perspective vanishing points) or simple isometric.

## When to Use

- Social media graphics (eye-catching at small sizes)
- Blog illustrations for abstract concepts
- Feature explanation graphics
- Onboarding screens and product UI illustrations
- Presentation slides where photography feels too heavy
- Email marketing headers
- Icon sets and spot illustrations

## When Not to Use

- When realism is important (use editorial or 3D render)
- When emotional depth is needed (use film still)
- When the subject requires detailed representation (technical photography)
- When the brand aesthetic is premium/luxury (tends to look too casual)

---

## Color Palette Guidance

For brand-aligned flat illustrations, take the brand's primary and secondary colors and build a limited palette:

1. **Primary**: Brand primary color (for the main subject/focal point)
2. **Secondary**: Brand secondary color (for supporting elements)
3. **Accent**: A complementary or contrasting color (for highlights and CTAs)
4. **Neutral**: Light gray or off-white (for backgrounds and non-essential elements)
5. **Dark**: Dark navy or charcoal (for text-like elements and outlines)

Avoid using more than 5 colors. Constraint creates visual consistency.
