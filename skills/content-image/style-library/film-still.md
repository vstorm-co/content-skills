# Film Still Style

## Description

Images that look like they were pulled from a feature film. Cinematic aspect ratios, dramatic lighting, intentional color grading, and atmospheric depth. Every frame tells a story. The visual language of Blade Runner, Wes Anderson, or a Coen Brothers film -- depending on the mood you need.

---

## Key Characteristics

- **Aspect ratio**: Cinematic wide (2.39:1 or 16:9). Avoid square crops.
- **Lighting**: Dramatic and intentional. Strong key light with visible shadows. Practical lighting sources visible in frame (lamps, screens, windows).
- **Color grading**: Heavy and deliberate. Teal-and-orange, desaturated cool, warm amber, or monochromatic -- pick one palette per image.
- **Grain**: Subtle film grain adds texture and analog feel. Not noise -- grain.
- **Depth**: Atmospheric haze, fog, or bokeh creates layers in the image.
- **Composition**: Cinematic framing. Leading lines, symmetry, or deliberate asymmetry. Subjects placed with intent.
- **Emotion**: Every image conveys a specific emotional state. Not neutral -- charged.

## Director-Inspired Variations

Use director references as style cues (the AI models recognize these):

| Director | Visual Language | Keywords |
|---|---|---|
| Wes Anderson | Symmetrical, pastel, whimsical, centered framing | "Wes Anderson style, symmetrical composition, pastel colors, centered framing" |
| Denis Villeneuve | Vast scale, desaturated, atmospheric, minimal | "Villeneuve style, vast landscape, desaturated, atmospheric, minimal composition" |
| David Fincher | Dark, green-tinted, precise, cold | "Fincher style, dark green tint, precise framing, cold and clinical" |
| Wong Kar-wai | Saturated, motion blur, neon, emotional | "Wong Kar-wai style, saturated colors, motion blur, neon-lit, romantic" |
| Roger Deakins | Natural light mastery, painterly, restrained | "Roger Deakins cinematography, natural light, painterly, restrained beauty" |

**Note**: Use director references as style guidance, not to copy specific frames. The goal is to capture the visual language, not reproduce copyrighted content.

## When to Use

- Hero images for thought leadership and opinion pieces
- Dramatic storytelling content
- Social media posts meant to stop the scroll
- Conference talk cover slides
- Brand campaigns with an emotional angle
- Feature announcements where you want gravitas

## When Not to Use

- Instructional or explanatory content (too atmospheric, not clear enough)
- Light and friendly brand personalities (feels too heavy)
- Quick-turn social content that needs to be on-brand but not dramatic
- Product documentation or technical content
